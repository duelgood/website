Mission

When you donate to a net-zero cause through DuelGood, your donation is matched with an equal opposing donation and both go to life-saving charities.

For example, if Alice donates $10 to the pro-life Cause A and Bob donates $8 to the pro-choice Cause B, then $8 + $8 = $16 goes to charity and the remaining $2 goes to Cause A.

Our operations are funded by donors who choose to support us. 100% of all other donations (minus standard bank and credit card fees) go to charity or the party of your choice.

Matched amounts go directly to GiveWell's Top Charities Fund, high-impact and cost-effective programs that save or improve lives the most per dollar. GiveWell is a highly transparent, reputable, and research-backed organization whose selected charities are the gold standard for impact. More about GiveWell.

The remainder will go to whichever cause received more donations of its pair.

Caveats

Many issues are supported by 501(c)(3)s on one side but by private groups, think tanks, political lobbying groups, or industry on the other. For instance there are virtually no anti-public transit charities, anti-union charities, anti-net neutrality, pro-factory farming, or anti-immigration charities, although there are certainly opposing groups working against one another in each of these issues. We deliberately sampled the largest/most-visible organizations. There are many smaller orgs, state-level groups, PACs, dark money channels, and pass-throughs that would raise the total if included. The result is a conservative lower-bound for the ten categories sampled. Many opposing dollars aren't in neat “pairings.” For example, Planned Parenthood is a network of national + affiliate charities + clinics; anti-abortion spending includes both political advocacy groups and a large number of independent pregnancy centers (the Guardian analysis tallies many of those centers). Some dollars are political (PACs), some are charitable (501(c)(3)), and some are 501(c)(4)/dark money - mixing them is messy but still informative if your goal is to estimate how much money is invested on both sides of a fight. “Wasted” is a value judgment. Donors and organizations typically view the spending as defending values or protecting rights; the word “wasted” here was used in the utilitarian sense of “resources whose net social effect cancels when both sides spend heavily.” We present raw revenue numbers so you can form your own judgment. (If you want a pure “net effect” analysis, we'd need outcome measures and causal impact studies - a much bigger research project.)
